# Loan and Trading Strategy Notes

## Overview
This note summarizes the working assumptions, cost model, and risk framework for using a Hong Kong personal loan as one-time trading capital for a July to September holding period with intended full repayment in October.[cite:55][cite:184]
The analysis assumes a target borrowing amount of HKD 705,000, which corresponds to 15 times a HKD 47,000 monthly salary.[cite:225][cite:224]
The user also expects salary to step up to HKD 55,000 by September, has no rent expense, and carries recurring monthly obligations of about HKD 7,000 for a ZA loan and HKD 3,000 for CRC costs.[cite:225][cite:218]

## Loan structure
OCBC publicly markets a personal loan product with a 60-day interest-free payment holiday and APRs starting from around 2.09%, with maximum borrowing up to 20 times salary or HKD 2 million, whichever is lower.[cite:13][cite:55]
OCBC's public service charge table does not state a universal personal-loan handling fee; instead, it says the handling fee is "based on loan agreement," which means the actual fee must be confirmed in the Key Facts Statement or loan agreement before signing.[cite:184]
For planning purposes, the analysis used a realistic handling-fee range of 1.0% to 2.0% of principal, with 1.5% treated as the central case rather than an assured offer.[cite:184]

## Cost model
If the loan is fully repaid within the 60-day interest-free window, the main direct cost is the handling fee rather than periodic interest.[cite:55]
Using a HKD 705,000 principal, the modeled fee outcomes are HKD 7,050 at 1.0%, HKD 10,575 at 1.5%, and HKD 14,100 at 2.0%.[cite:224]
That implies break-even portfolio returns of 1.0%, 1.5%, and 2.0% respectively over the holding period, before any profit is retained.[cite:224]
Because the first repayment is tied to the product's payment-holiday structure, the working assumption in the later model was that the full HKD 705,000 remains the investable base if personal salary covers living expenses during the holding period.[cite:55][cite:225]

## Profit and loss scenarios
Using the central 1.5% fee assumption, a 5% gain on HKD 705,000 produces gross profit of HKD 35,250 and net profit of HKD 24,675 after the fee.[cite:224]
Under the same assumption, a 10% gain produces HKD 70,500 gross and HKD 59,925 net, while a 20% gain produces HKD 141,000 gross and HKD 130,425 net.[cite:224]
On the downside, a 10% loss produces a gross trading loss of HKD 70,500 and a total net loss of HKD 81,075 after the 1.5% fee.[cite:224]
A 20% loss under the same fee assumption would produce a total net loss of HKD 151,575, which is roughly 2.8 months of a HKD 55,000 monthly salary.[cite:225][cite:224]

## Cashflow and headroom
The target downside condition was to preserve about HKD 30,000 of operating headroom after fixed obligations.[cite:220]
With salary at HKD 55,000 from September and fixed obligations of HKD 10,000 per month, preserving that headroom is materially easier if the trade is repaid in full in October rather than held over a long amortization schedule.[cite:225][cite:218]
The dialogue also concluded that, because there is no rent and low living-cost pressure, it is more sensible to treat monthly salary as the buffer for operating expenses rather than haircutting the trading capital unnecessarily.[cite:225]

## Risk framework
The strategy only makes sense if it is run with explicit entry, stop-loss, and exit rules rather than discretionary averaging-down.[cite:257][cite:267][cite:272]
A practical framework discussed in the dialogue was to tolerate about a 2% normal adverse move as volatility noise, but pair that with a hard stop around 10% on the full-position trade so that downside remains capped.[cite:257][cite:272]
With a full-size HKD 705,000 position and a 1.5% fee, a 2% adverse move implies a trading loss of HKD 14,100 and a total loss of HKD 24,675 after fees, while a 10% stop implies a total loss of HKD 81,075.[cite:224]
That makes the one-time trade attractive only if the expected upside is in the 15% to 30% range and the stop discipline is actually followed.[cite:224]

## Market backdrop
Recent reporting in June 2026 shows that equity performance has remained heavily concentrated around the AI theme, with investor attention focused on AI infrastructure, memory, and data-center buildout.[cite:274]
That backdrop supports the thesis that a correctly timed AI trade could plausibly generate 10% to 30% upside over a three-month window, but it does not remove the possibility of sharp drawdowns in equally volatile names.[cite:274][cite:281]
Because of that asymmetry, the trade was framed as a one-off, high-convexity bet rather than a repeatable financing loop.[cite:221]

## Main conclusion
The most realistic framing is a one-time HKD 705,000 trade funded by an OCBC personal loan, with actual handling fee to be confirmed in the Key Facts Statement, realistic planning centered on a 1.5% fee, and full repayment targeted within the product's payment-holiday window.[cite:55][cite:184]
At that cost level, the trade breaks even at about 1.5%, becomes meaningfully attractive around 15% to 20% upside, and remains survivable on the downside only if stop-loss discipline is respected.[cite:224]
The single most important unknown remains the actual fee and settlement wording in OCBC's final loan documents, so the final go/no-go decision depends on the Key Facts Statement rather than public marketing language alone.[cite:184][cite:55]
